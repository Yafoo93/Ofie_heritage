A Journey Through Ghana’s Historical Sites: Beyond the Castles

1. Fort St. Jago: A Strategic StrongholdLocated on a hill overlooking Elmina Castle, Fort St. Jago was built by the Dutch in the 17th century as a strategic stronghold to protect their interests in the region. Unlike Elmina Castle, which was primarily used for trade, Fort St. Jago served a military purpose, offering a defensive position with sweeping views of the coastline. Today, Fort St. Jago stands as a reminder of the intense competition between European powers for control of the lucrative trade routes along the West African coast. Visitors to the fort can explore its well-preserved ramparts, learn about its role in the colonial history of Ghana, and enjoy panoramic views of Elmina and the surrounding area.

2. Manhyia Palace: The Seat of the Ashanti Kingdom In the heart of Kumasi, the cultural capital of Ghana, lies Manhyia Palace, the official residence of the Asantehene, the king of the Ashanti people. The Ashanti Kingdom was one of the most powerful and influential states in West Africa, known for its wealth, military prowess, and rich cultural heritage. The original palace was built by the British in 1925 for King Prempeh I, and today it houses a museum that offers a fascinating glimpse into the history and traditions of the Ashanti people. Visitors can explore the palace grounds, view royal regalia, and learn about the Ashanti’s significant role in Ghana’s history. The museum’s exhibits include artefacts, photographs, and documents that chronicle the dynasty of Ashanti kings, from the founding of the kingdom to the present day.

3. Larabanga Mosque: Ghana’s Oldest Mosque Larabanga Mosque, located in the Northern Region of Ghana, is the oldest mosque in the country and one of the oldest in West Africa. Built in the Sudanese architectural style, this small, whitewashed structure is made of mud and stick, with a unique design that includes pyramidal towers and spiked buttresses. The mosque is believed to have been built in the 15th century and has been a centre of Islamic worship in the region for centuries. It is also known as the “Mecca of West Africa” due to its significance in Islamic history. Visitors to Larabanga Mosque can admire its striking architecture, learn about its history, and experience the spiritual atmosphere that has made it a revered site for both locals and pilgrims.

4. Salaga Slave Market: A Sobering Reminder of the Past The town of Salaga, located in the Northern Region, was once a major centre for the trans-Saharan slave trade. Known as the “Timbuktu of Ghana,” Salaga was a bustling market town where enslaved people from across West Africa were bought and sold before being transported to coastal forts for shipment across the Atlantic. Today, the remnants of the Salaga Slave Market serve as a sobering reminder of this dark chapter in history. Visitors can see the slave wells, where captives were bathed and held before being auctioned, and the market site itself, which still bears traces of its past. A visit to Salaga offers a powerful and emotional experience, providing important context to the transatlantic slave trade and its impact on Ghana.

5. Nzulezo: The Village on Stilts Nzulezo is a unique historical site in Ghana, located on Lake Tadane in the Western Region. This village is entirely built on stilts over the water, and it is believed to have been established in the 16th century by settlers fleeing conflict in their homeland. Nzulezo has been recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its cultural significance and its adaptation to the watery environment. Visitors to Nzulezo can reach the village by canoe, paddling through the lush mangroves that surround the lake. Once in the village, you’ll experience a way of life that has remained largely unchanged for centuries. The community is self-sufficient, relying on fishing, farming, and trading to sustain itself. Nzulezo is not just a fascinating historical site but also a living example of resilience and ingenuity.

6. Osu Castle: A Seat of Power Osu Castle, also known as Christiansborg Castle, is a significant historical landmark located in Accra. Originally built by the Danes in the 17th century, the castle has served as the seat of government for both colonial and post-independence Ghana. Throughout its history, Osu Castle has been a symbol of political power and control, and it has undergone several transformations as different European powers vied for dominance in the region. Today, Osu Castle is a government property and is occasionally open to the public for tours. Visitors can explore its colonial architecture, learn about its role in Ghana’s political history, and reflect on its complex legacy as both a centre of governance and a former site of slave trading activities.

7. Aburi Botanical Gardens: A Colonial-Era Retreat Aburi Botanical Gardens, located in the Eastern Region of Ghana, is a lush, tranquil space that dates back to the colonial era. Established in 1890 by British colonialists, the gardens were originally intended as an agricultural research station and a place for colonial officials to retreat from the heat of the coast. Today, the gardens are a popular destination for both locals and tourists, offering a peaceful environment with a wide variety of tropical plants, towering trees, and beautiful landscapes. Visitors can take guided tours to learn about the history of the gardens, the medicinal properties of various plants, and the colonial legacy that still lingers in this serene space.

8. The Navrongo Cathedral: A Blend of Christianity and Tradition In the Upper East Region of Ghana, the Navrongo Cathedral, also known as Our Lady of Seven Sorrows, stands as a testament to the fusion of Christianity and traditional African culture. Built in 1906, the cathedral is one of the oldest Catholic churches in Ghana and is unique for its blend of European and local architectural styles. The cathedral’s mud-brick walls are adorned with traditional motifs, and the interior features murals that depict biblical scenes alongside images of local culture and history. The Navrongo Cathedral is not just a place of worship but also a cultural landmark that reflects the adaptation of Christianity to the local context in northern Ghana.

9. Pikworo Slave Camp: A Site of Resistance Pikworo Slave Camp, located near Paga in the Upper East Region, was established in 1704 as a holding area for enslaved Africans before they were marched to the coast for sale. Unlike other slave sites, Pikworo is significant for its role in the resistance against the slave trade. It is believed that many captives attempted to escape or resist their captors while being held at this camp. Visitors to Pikworo can see the rock formations that served as eating places, the ponds where captives were forced to drink from, and the rocks used for communication among the captives. The site offers a poignant glimpse into the harsh realities of the slave trade and the resilience of those who were captured.

10. Shai Hills Resource Reserve: A Glimpse into Ancient Settlements Shai Hills Resource Reserve, located just outside Accra, is a protected area that offers both natural beauty and historical significance. The reserve is home to the Shai people, who once lived in the caves within the hills. These caves served as natural fortresses and homes, and they hold evidence of ancient settlements, including pottery, tools, and other artefacts. Visitors to Shai Hills can hike through the reserve, explore the caves, and learn about the history and culture of the Shai people. The reserve is also home to a variety of wildlife, including baboons, antelopes, and birds, making it a great destination for nature lovers and history enthusiasts alike.
